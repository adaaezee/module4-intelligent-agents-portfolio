Reflection

The following reflection draws on my experiences throughout the Intelligent Agents module, structured using Rolfe et al.'s (2001) reflective framework: What? (description), So What? (analysis), and Now What? (action).

Throughout this module, I have gained not only theoretical knowledge encompassing intelligent agents and their real-world applications, but also a level of technical expertise I had not anticipated. As a creative professional with an analytical inclination, I was eager to engage with the Intelligent Agents module, which marked my fourth module on this course.

What?

The module spanned twelve weeks and covered a broad range of topics, from the foundations of agent-based computing and BDI architectures to agent communication languages and the ethical implications of generative AI. Key assessments included a collaborative group project, individual presentation, and a series of collaborative discussion forums across Units 1 to 11.

So What?

Working on the group project across Units 1 to 6 was a significant learning experience. My previous experiences of group work had not always been positive, often characterised by inconsistent communication and unequal contributions. I was therefore apprehensive at the outset. This apprehension was rooted in prior experiences where unequal contribution had placed disproportionate pressure on individual members, ultimately affecting the quality of the final output. Entering the project with this mindset meant I was particularly conscious of pulling my weight and communicating proactively. In hindsight, this awareness positively shaped my approach - I was more deliberate in taking ownership of tasks and more attentive to team dynamics than I might otherwise have been. However, Group E proved to be an exception - my teammates Richard, Sarah and Ioanna were collaborative, communicative and dependable, and we were able to assign responsibilities according to individual strengths and experience. Drawing on my background as a Marketing Manager, I naturally gravitated towards research and visual communication, taking responsibility for the UML architecture diagrams for our multi-agent BDI system.

Prior to this project, I had no practical experience of modelling how agents interact with one another and their environment. The reading resource by Russell and Norvig (2021) in Unit 2 and the lecturecast on Agent Architecture in Unit 3 were particularly valuable in developing my understanding of first-order logic and the distinction between intentions and desires - concepts that were critical to the UML design process. Wooldridge (2009) further grounded my understanding of multi-agent systems as a technology and their real-world applications.

The tutor feedback on the group project was encouraging and constructive, achieving a grade of 76%. Reflecting on this critically, the feedback highlighted that diagram labelling could be improved and that deeper comparison with alternative agent architectures would have strengthened the analysis. This was a fair observation and one I have taken note of for future work. Designing the BDI system also gave me a practical understanding of the content I had explored in Collaborative Discussion 1, where I used the SimMobility platform as a real-world example of multi-agent coordination in urban transport planning. Experiencing the architecture from a design perspective made that example far more tangible (Adnan et al., 2016).

The individual presentation that followed the group project allowed me to consolidate this learning further, demonstrating through live testing how design decisions within the BDI system translated into system behaviour, and reinforcing my understanding of the implementation mechanisms that the group report had touched upon at a higher level.

The collaborative discussion forums, which ran across Units 1 to 9, were another highlight of the module. I have always found forum-based learning engaging, as it encourages me to connect theory with real-world practice and broaden my perspective through peer interaction. Discussion 2, which focused on agent communication languages such as KQML, was particularly valuable from a technical standpoint. Exploring how agents communicate across heterogeneous systems and comparing this with method invocation in Python deepened my understanding of distributed system design - knowledge that directly informed my appreciation of the architectural decisions made during the group project. A post by Elsam A, which approached generative AI from a philosophical angle with a focus on its impact on creative industries, was particularly thought-provoking and prompted me to extend my own research into the ethical implications of tools such as DALL-E. This culminated in Discussion 3, which I found to be the most personally relevant of the three forums. Researching generative AI across healthcare, digital arts and education deepened my awareness of the ethical risks associated with these technologies, including concerns around bias, misinformation and intellectual property (European Commission, no date). This is directly applicable to my professional practice, where AI-generated imagery is increasingly used in marketing materials - an area I now approach with considerably more critical awareness.

Now What?

On a personal level, managing this module alongside full-time work, international events and medical appointments across the final months was challenging. I am conscious that this affected my engagement with the latter units and limited my attendance at seminars, which I value as a space for discussion and peer connection. Moving forwards, I intend to be more proactive in communicating module commitments to my employer and in managing competing demands, as I am committed to pursuing a career in artificial intelligence and wish to engage as fully as possible with my remaining modules.

Looking ahead, one area I am keen to explore further is deepfake regulation. A widely reported incident involving manipulated footage of a public figure demonstrated how convincingly synthetic media can distort reality and erode public trust (Patel et al., 2023). Understanding the technical and regulatory landscape surrounding deepfakes feels both timely and relevant to the intersection of AI ethics and my professional field. As someone aspiring to transition into a technical role within artificial intelligence, developing a critical understanding of both the capabilities and risks of emerging technologies is essential. I intend to pursue this through continued self-directed study, engaging with current research and regulatory developments, and applying the analytical and technical skills developed in this module to future projects and professional practice.

Overall, the Intelligent Agents module has significantly enriched my understanding of agent-based systems and their application across domains including healthcare, transport and education. It has also reinforced the importance of ethical awareness in the development and deployment of AI technologies - a principle I will carry forward into both my studies and professional practice.
