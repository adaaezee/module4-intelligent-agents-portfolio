Units 9–11: Collaborative Discussion 3

Topic: Deep Learning - The advent of new technologies supported by Deep Learning models mean that it is now possible to generate 'new' content, for example, DALL-E AI to generate images or ChatGPT to create prose. Do you think that these new technologies offer any ethical issues that should be considered, and if not, why not?

This discussion ran across Units 9–11 and required an initial post, at least two peer responses, and a summary post. References in UoEO Harvard style were required throughout.

Initial Post

Initial Post • Unit 9

The rapid development of artificial intelligence has driven the emergence of new technologies such as large language models (LLMs), with ChatGPT being a prominent example used across multiple disciplines. ChatGPT is a transformer-based model capable of processing vast amounts of data, enabling it to understand and generate human-like text and produce convincing, grammatically fluent prose comparable to that written by human authors (Alberts et al., 2023).

In parallel, AI image generation tools have also experienced significant growth, particularly those developed under DALL-E. These systems can generate highly realistic images from textual descriptions and are often integrated into platforms such as ChatGPT, expanding possibilities for creativity and content generation (Zhou & Nabus, 2023). However, these capabilities also introduce important ethical concerns.

One significant issue is the potential misuse of generative AI in the fabrication of scientific and medical content. Image generation models such as DALL-E can produce highly realistic visuals from prompts, raising concerns about the manipulation of research data. This is particularly problematic in medical imaging, where evidence suggests that image manipulation already exists and may become increasingly difficult to detect as AI technologies advance. The ability to generate convincing synthetic medical images therefore poses a risk to research integrity and could contribute to the spread of misleading or false information (Shen et al., 2023).

Additionally, concerns have been raised regarding bias in AI systems. According to BBC News (2024), the NHS has highlighted that while generative AI has the potential to improve patient outcomes, it may also reinforce existing health inequalities, particularly where training data does not adequately represent diverse populations. This can result in biased outputs that disproportionately affect certain groups. To address these challenges, the implementation of ethical frameworks and regulatory oversight is essential. Ensuring transparency, improving dataset diversity, and maintaining human oversight are key steps in mitigating the ethical risks associated with generative AI technologies.

Peer Responses

Unit 10 • My Peer Responses

Peer Response 1 - Response to Elsam

Hello Elsam, as a creative individual, I found your post particularly thought-provoking, especially your exploration of the philosophical implications of generative AI and how it challenges our understanding of human creativity.

Your discussion highlights an important distinction between human and artificial creativity, and the limitations that may exist within AI-generated outputs. Building on this perspective, it is also important to consider how these developments impact creative industries in practice. While generative AI can produce content in a wide range of styles, this raises questions around authorship and the value of human creativity. For example, in fields such as digital art and writing, widely used tools such as DALL-E and Stable Diffusion have demonstrated their ability to generate compelling visual content.

However, the use of AI-generated content introduces ethical concerns, particularly in relation to ownership and originality. Generated outputs may replicate stylistic elements of human creators without clear attribution, making it difficult to determine authorship and potentially undermining the value of original creative work (Mitkus et al., 2023). This reinforces your point that generative AI not only challenges how creativity is defined, but also how it is recognised and valued in practice.

Peer Response 2 - Response to Gayathridevi

Thank you for your post, Gayathridevi! I found your discussion on the ethical implications of generative AI, particularly within education and online media, both insightful and relevant.

Reflecting on your points, I was encouraged to explore further research into how generative AI is being applied in practice. While these technologies present ethical risks related to data privacy, misinformation, and reliability, they are also being embraced as transformative tools by major institutions. For example, the Department for Education (DfE) recognises the potential of generative AI to enhance educational practices and actively promotes its safe, responsible, and effective use. Notably, the DfE provides guidance on data protection in schools and colleges, emphasising the importance of safeguarding personal data in line with existing legislation (GOV.UK, 2025).

Moreover, in higher education, AI technologies are increasingly being used to support teaching and learning. Intelligent tutoring systems, for instance, can assist educators by providing automated feedback and assessment, enhancing both efficiency and accessibility. Image generation tools such as DALL-E further extend these capabilities by introducing new methods of engagement. Research has shown that AI-generated visual content can stimulate curiosity and encourage discussion, particularly in areas such as vocabulary learning, leading to increased student interaction and active learning (Fu & Weng, 2024; Fannoni et al., 2023).

Therefore, while generative AI presents important ethical challenges, particularly in educational contexts, it is equally important to recognise its potential benefits. A balanced approach that combines ethical awareness with responsible implementation will ensure that these technologies are used to enhance, rather than undermine, learning outcomes. This complements your argument by highlighting that, alongside ethical concerns, generative AI also presents meaningful opportunities when applied responsibly.

Summary Post

Summary Post • Unit 11

In summary, engaging with my peers' posts has provided valuable insight into the emergence of generative AI technologies and their ethical implications across a range of domains.

My initial post explored the transformative impact of generative AI, with a particular focus on AI-generated content in healthcare. Research conducted by the University of Oxford highlights the risks associated with AI systems, particularly chatbots providing medical advice, identifying issues such as inaccurate and inconsistent information. This reinforces the importance of placing ethical considerations at the centre of these technologies, especially in high-stakes environments where reliability is critical (University of Oxford, 2026).

Similar ethical concerns were also evident in other domains discussed by my peers. For example, Elsam's post on creative industries highlighted how generative AI challenges traditional notions of creativity, raising questions around authorship, originality, and the value of human-generated work. This demonstrates that ethical concerns are not limited to a single domain, but instead extend across both technical and creative fields. To address these challenges, regulatory frameworks such as the EU AI Act have been introduced, emphasising the importance of data governance, transparency, and accountability in the development and deployment of AI systems. These frameworks aim to ensure that AI technologies are used responsibly, particularly in sensitive sectors such as healthcare (European Commission, no date).

Overall, while technologies such as DALL-E offer significant opportunities for innovation and efficiency, they also present risks related to bias, misinformation, and harmful outputs. It is therefore essential to ensure that these systems are developed and deployed in a way that prioritises reliability, transparency, and user safety (Tian et al., 2024).
